How I got into tech

I wrote my first "program" in seventh grade math class: a set of instructions to move a mouse cursor along a computer screen so that it drew various shapes (we were learning about angles).

But my first problem-solving and logic "training", if you could call it that, was in elementary school. I was an oddball: I knew how to read before I started kindergarten. So in the first grade, while my peers were learning how to read, they had me sit in a room by myself with a printed stack of brain teaser puzzles. They were usually patten matching puzzles, or the kind where you draw a line connecting all the dots on the page without crossing a line. That sort of thing. There was no grade involved, no teacher to verify that I either did or didn't solve the puzzle (I knew I always did). There were probably at least twenty pages in that puzzle stack and I didn't know how many I was supposed to solve per day, etc. This resulted in a dislike for doing brain teasers just for the sake of doing them. The only thing that part of my life brought me was the dubious "honor" of skipping second grade.

So returning back to seventh grade, I found myself in the exact same situation. I'd finish math early and end up doing "extra credit" assignments, which were, you guessed it, more brain teasers. I got a gold star on the calendar for each day that I solved the brain teaser.

In college, my degree had a couple of "specializations" (not minors). One was business, the other was math. I chose the business one because I was more interested in how people use technology in their daily lives. The math speciality was more geared to compiler writing, systems, and graphics. I didn't find those as interesting at the time, plus it scared me, to be honest, that I could write the software that people's lives depended on. I could imagine the amount of stress involved in programmning airplane navigation or hospital laser treatment machines. At least in business, the most you can do is lose money, not lives.

Fast-forward to the present. I'm a software engineer, having earned a B.S. in Computing and Information Sciences. I have enough years of experience to be considered "mid" or "senior". How did I break into tech, then? I went the CS degree route. I did an internship my sophomore year and worked at the university's help desk my junior and senior years. I signed up for on-campus interviews my senior year, and I had accepted a job offer before finals. The job offer was for a 6-week paid bootcamp at the job site, with a final project. If your final project passed, you would move into a permanent (associate) role. I'm pretty sure everyone in that bootcamp passed; it wasn't a very hard project. Et voil√†!

I've gone my entire career without having to solve a single "Leetcode" problem! In fact it was only last year (2023) when I even heard of Leetcode. I hear that it's required a lot especially in today's job market. So, back to brainteasers all over again, whereas most if not all the code I've written has been to solve a specific business problems.
